Fail On Purpose

Rosovo  ·  Dec 27, 2025

A lot of people around you are going to assume creativity works a certain way. Friends, family, basically anyone watching from the outside tends to think people are either born with talent or they're not. That you're either gifted at something or you're just lucky enough to have it "click." And if it doesn't click early, the assumption is that it probably won't click at all. I don't think that's true in any meaningful way.

Most good work doesn't come from being naturally good at something. It comes from being willing to make a lot of bad work first. Not in a symbolic sense, but in a very literal one. You don't start with 100 good ideas and refine them into something great. You start with ideas that barely work, you push them into reality, and most of them fail in obvious ways. That failure isn't a side effect of the process. It is the process.

I write articles, I make music, I draw. Across all three, the ratio looks about the same. Somewhere around 90 percent of what I make gets thrown out or shoved into an archive folder I'll probably never open again. A draft that never leaves the notes app. A track that gets three layers deep before I realize it was never going anywhere. A drawing where the hand is wrong in a way I can't fix without starting over. None of that is a sign something went wrong. It's just what the process actually looks like once you stop editing the story of it after the fact.

If I say I want to make 100 of something, whether that's songs, drawings, or drafts, I'm not saying I expect 100 I'm proud of. I'm saying I expect most of them to be bad, and that's completely fine. That expectation is actually what makes the whole thing work. Because the goal isn't to protect the quality of every individual output, it's to create enough outputs that something interesting eventually appears inside the noise. People tend to misunderstand this part and think the bad work means something is going wrong, when in reality it's the only reason anything good shows up at all.

Each failed attempt still has value, just not in the way people usually think. It's not building toward success in a clean, linear way. It's more like you're exposing yourself to your own thinking under pressure. You find out what doesn't translate, what collapses when it leaves your head, what only works in theory. A sentence that sounded sharp in your head reads flat on the page. A chord progression that felt inevitable while you were humming it falls apart the second it's recorded. Over time, you stop seeing these as individual failures and start seeing them as patterns in your output. That's where improvement actually happens, not in inspiration or sudden clarity, but in repeated exposure to your own mistakes until they become predictable.

The other part people miss is how much of this is blocked by perception. Most people don't avoid making things because they're incapable of it. They avoid it because they don't want to be seen as someone who makes bad work. It feels like it says something about them. So they stay in planning, or they keep refining ideas before anything exists, or they wait until they feel "ready." But that readiness never really arrives, because it depends on doing the thing they're avoiding.

The truth is that anyone can do anything, but only if they're willing to go through the part where they're bad at it for a while. That part isn't optional. It's not a sign you're on the wrong path. It's the entry fee. And once you accept that, the whole thing becomes much simpler. You make the thing, you see what doesn't work, you make it again, and slowly the ratio shifts. Good work doesn't appear because you waited until you were ready. It appears because you stopped stopping yourself.

That's really all this is. Not talent, not luck, not some hidden ability that only a few people have. Just repetition without protecting yourself from the early results. Ninety percent of it ends up in the trash or the archive either way. The other ten percent is the only reason any of this was worth doing in the first place.
