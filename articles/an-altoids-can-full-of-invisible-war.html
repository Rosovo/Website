An Altoids Can Full of Invisible Conflict

Rosovo  ·  May 29, 2026

I found it on the ground outside my apartment in Riverview, early morning, nobody around. An Altoids can. I have not seen anybody actually eating Altoids since maybe middle school. It was heavy in a way empty tins are not, and curiosity is a hard thing to argue with at six in the morning.

It was dented. Bent metal flaps on the sides, the kind you would pry with a fingernail if you knew they were there. Corroded shut, like it had taken on water at some point and forgotten how to open. I sat there for a second wondering what was inside, such an odd looking can of mints, turning it over, working a fingernail into the seam. After a couple minutes of prying it eventually cracked, and I got it open, and what I saw inside was so unnatural I can tell you right now, it was not Altoids.

Glued to the lid, superglue and a fair amount of optimism, was a tiny LCD screen. Glued to the bottom, a little motherboard, half the tin's real estate, with a Raspberry Pi stamped on the chip. Next to it, more glue, more wires, things I was pretty sure were antennas and sensors. Two ports coming off the board, one a normal USB-C, one a strange little grid of holes I did not recognize. And taped to the inner wall, duct tape and Sharpie, one word: KALI .

I did not know what any of it meant yet. I knew it was not an Altoids tin anymore.

Here is the thing about every piece of that little device. None of it was born dangerous. The Raspberry Pi was built so a kid in a bedroom could learn to code, that is the company's entire reason for existing. The tin held mints. The superglue holds picture frames together. The duct tape patches tents and ducts and, apparently, labels homemade hacking tools. KALI, I would learn, is the name of a Linux operating system built for security testing, used by actual professionals at actual companies who get paid to break into networks on purpose. Every ingredient in that can was invented for something small and decent. Somebody just pointed it somewhere else.

I took it to two friends who do this for a living. Pentesters , the official term, people companies hire to hack their own systems before someone worse does it for free. I will call them Buddy A and Buddy B, because neither of them wants their name in an article about a thing found on a sidewalk.

Buddy A took one look and shrugged. Pi Zero, he said, probably running Kali off a microSD card that is long dead from water damage. The weird port is GPIO , standard headers, you solder wires into it if you are building something custom. This is a hobby project. Somebody's school assignment, or somebody's weekend, or somebody's first real attempt at learning how Wi-Fi sniffing works. It is a toy. Cute that you found it. Not worth losing sleep over.

Buddy B disagreed, and not gently. Does not matter what it was for, he said. What matters is what it can do, and what it can do is sit in a coffee shop or an office lobby and quietly log every device that walks past, or impersonate a trusted network and watch what people type. He wanted to bag it, run it in an isolated environment, check for logs, see if there was anything on it that looked like it had actually been used on a real target. Toy or not, he said, you do not just assume.

They went back and forth for a while. Hobbyist project versus possible surveillance tool. Nothing to see here versus we should be careful. And somewhere in the middle of that argument, I stopped caring who was right, because both of them were arguing about a tin can.

Here is what neither of them brought up once, the entire time, because it did not occur to either of them to bring it up. While Buddy A and Buddy B argued over an Altoids can that maybe sniffed Wi-Fi in a parking lot, somewhere else entirely, governments are bombing data centers .

In March 2026, Iranian drones struck Amazon Web Services facilities in the United Arab Emirates and Bahrain , damaging physical infrastructure and disrupting cloud services across the region. Not a hack. Missiles. For the first time in modern conflict, commercial hyperscale data centers became explicit kinetic targets, and security researchers are calling it a watershed, the moment cloud infrastructure stopped being an abstraction and became something you could put a crater in. Cloud reliability is engineered to manage component failures and outages, not to withstand missiles or drone strikes, which is a polite way of saying nobody designed for this, because two years ago nobody thought they would need to.

That is the scale gap. My tin is a guy with a glue gun. The other end of this is a government deciding a data center is worth bombing.

The part that actually unsettled me was not the bombs, though. It was the software. The UK's National Cyber Security Centre has put it at 95 to 100 percent confidence that by 2027, general-purpose AI systems will make cyber offense more effective and efficient, which is about as close as a government agency gets to saying the quiet part out loud. In a DARPA-run competition designed specifically to test this, one AI system found 77 percent of synthetic software vulnerabilities and patched 61 percent of them across 54 million lines of code. No human team reads that in a weekend.

Sit with that for a second, because I had to. The same report notes that the window to fix a publicly disclosed vulnerability has already shrunk to days in some cases, a window that used to be measured in weeks, sometimes months, the gap where defenders used to have time to breathe. Industry researchers are projecting something stranger for this year: that 15 percent of observed zero day exploits could be discovered and weaponized by autonomous agents before human researchers even finish categorizing the vulnerability. Machines finding the hole and building the weapon before the people whose job it is to name the hole have finished naming it.

I want to be careful here, because this is exactly where these pieces usually go off the rails into something that sounds like a trailer for a movie that does not exist. The AI tools you or I can actually open right now, the consumer chatbots, are built with guardrails specifically so they will not hand you working malware no matter how you ask. That part of the panic is overstated. What is not overstated is slower and less cinematic: AI lowering the floor for less skilled attackers, automating the boring repetitive reconnaissance that used to take a human days, helping a state-backed group move faster against a vulnerability than the people defending it can patch. It is not Skynet. It is a much faster pair of hands doing the same old job.

Meanwhile, the actual money makes the gap obvious in a different way. Around $2.9 trillion is projected to be spent on data centers supporting AI between 2025 and 2029, entire regions being built around megawatts of compute. That is the laser gun. My tin, water damaged, held together with hot glue, run by someone who will never be identified and probably does not think about it anymore, is the bow and arrow. Buddy A and Buddy B were arguing about whether the arrow was sharp. Nobody in that argument was talking about the laser, because the laser was not sitting on the table in front of us. It never is. That is sort of the point.

I still do not know who built the tin, or why, or whether he is some kid who got bored on a Saturday or someone who actually used it on someone. I will never know. He is gone the way a dropped glove is gone, an object with no story attached.

I still have the tin. It still does not turn on. I have decided I like it better that way.
