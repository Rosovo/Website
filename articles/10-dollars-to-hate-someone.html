$10 to Hate Someone

Rosovo  ·  June 29, 2026

A parlay goes wrong, Ronaldo takes the blame, and somewhere in the middle of it all, something starts to feel less like a game.

"Fuck you, Ronaldo! You owe me ten dollars!" That's what I actually shouted, out loud, alone in my house, at a television. My parlay was one leg off. He missed an open net in the 81st minute and somehow that felt personal. I threw my phone on the couch and said I was done. Twenty minutes later I picked it back up, not even to bet, just to do my daily free spins. They give you three a day. It is extraordinarily hard to get even twenty cents out of them, let alone anything more, but I do it every single day anyway, World Cup or no World Cup, money on the line or not.

It wasn't until I caught myself mid-rant at a man who has never met me that I realized the problem wasn't ten dollars. It was ten dollars, many separate times, so many that I had stopped counting. People ask me how I'm doing and sometimes I'll say, half joking, that I'm just out here losing my sports bets. I mean it as a joke. I don't think anything of it. But I've started noticing the look on someone's face when they don't take it that way, when "ha, I'm losing money gambling" lands less like a bit and more like a confession, and they start wondering if I'm actually fine.

Here's the thing that makes it weirder: I love Cristiano Ronaldo. Genuinely. He's my favorite football player of all time, and when I say football, I mean soccer, for anyone reading this who's American and confused. Growing up he was something close to a role model. "Talent without working hard is nothing" is one of his, and it's the kind of thing that actually stuck with me as a kid, the discipline of it, the idea that none of it comes free. He made me want to take care of myself. He's never done anything to me. But I needed somewhere to put the anger, and it couldn't go where it actually belonged. If I got mad at the app, at the habit, at whatever this actually was, I'd have to admit it was a problem. And I didn't want it to be a problem, because some part of it still felt good. So Ronaldo ate it instead. Every missed leg, every blown parlay, all of it landed on a guy who was just doing his job.

I want to be clear about what this isn't. I was never addicted, not in the clinical sense. I never touched my entire paycheck. Most of the time it was social. FIFA's on, friends are over, everyone's got a few dollars on the game, it's part of the ritual the same way snacks and a group chat are. Some years I came out ahead, barely, which I now know is the exception and not the rule. But I'd be lying if I said it was purely seasonal. I open the app for the free spins every single day, World Cup or no World Cup. So maybe "it's just a World Cup thing" isn't entirely true. Maybe it's just the version of this that's designed to feel optional.

That's what got me looking into it more. I'd heard somewhere, YouTube I think, take it with a grain of salt, that gambling addicts have something like the highest suicide rate of any addiction out there. I wasn't sure if that was true, so I went looking to confirm it before I wrote it down here as fact. What I found wasn't exactly the number I'd heard, but it didn't need to be exaggerated to be bad. Problem gambling has the highest suicide attempt rate of any addiction that's been studied. Not the highest among gamblers. The highest among every addiction researchers have looked at, period, ahead of alcohol, ahead of opioids, ahead of everything. One in five problem gamblers attempts suicide. One in five! Sit with that number for a second, because I had to. That's not a stat I needed to round up to make it land.

And it's not really about the money, or at least not only about the money. That's the part that surprised me most while looking into this. Wealthy people lose to this exact same addiction. People with more money than they could ever burn through still get pulled into the loop, still lose and lose, still spiral, and some of them still kill themselves over it. So whatever this is, it's not a poverty problem. It's not a math problem you can solve by having more money to lose. Something about the loop itself, the near miss, the one-leg-off parlay, the spin that almost hits, does something to a brain regardless of what's in the bank account behind it.

The part that scares me more than my own ten dollars is how young this is starting, and how little anyone with the power to stop it seems to actually want to. There isn't one single federal minimum-age standard for sports betting; it's mostly state-by-state, which means in some places an eighteen-year-old can open an account as easily as he can order an Uber. There's nothing close to a real ID check stopping someone younger than that from logging into a parent's account, or just making a fake one. A bill that would have banned betting ads during live games, required real "affordability checks" on big spenders, and blocked companies from using AI to target people who gamble more than they should got introduced in Congress. It stalled. Meanwhile the same companies are spending money backing candidates ahead of this year's midterms, which, in betting terms, is what you'd call a tell. They know something's coming and they're trying to make sure it doesn't land on them.

I thought the scary part was how easy it is to bet on sports. Then I learned sports betting isn't even the outer edge anymore.

FanDuel and DraftKings don't need "prediction markets" to make gambling feel infinite. Their core sportsbook apps already let you bet on basically anything: who wins, who scores, how many points/goals/saves, and a million tiny in-game moments that are over fast enough to feel like pulling a lever.

But the prediction-market world is where it gets genuinely weird. Apps like Kalshi and Polymarket let people trade on outcomes that look and feel like bets, but they're framed as "event contracts" and "trading." Weather. Elections. Court cases. Culture. Sports. Anything you can attach a number to. And when the thing you're betting on is a sensor reading or some real-world disruption, it starts creating incentives for people to mess with reality itself. There have already been investigations into suspected tampering with a weather sensor near a major airport tied to prediction-market temperature bets. And on the sports side, prediction markets have even hosted markets around disruptive stuff happening in games, which is a sentence that should make everyone feel insane for a second.

Slot machines used to require a building, a drive, a reason to go. Now it's a phone in your pocket, three free spins waiting for you when you wake up, and a wide-open door for anyone who can type in a birthday.

I don't have a clean ending for this, because I don't think there's a clean version of the actual problem either. But I know what would help. Real federal age verification instead of an honor system. A ban on ads during live games instead of a bill that stalled in Congress while the same companies write checks to the people supposed to be regulating them. Actual oversight on whatever Kalshi and the rest of the prediction markets are calling themselves this week so they don't get to dodge addiction protections just by renaming the product. None of that is radical. It's the bare minimum, and right now we don't even have that.

I'm not in trouble. I know people who are. I know the version of this that's "fine" still made me scream at a soccer player I admire, over a number small enough that I shouldn't have noticed it at all. If that's what the safe version looks like, I don't really want to know what the other one feels like from the inside. I already watched it happen to people I care about, and it wasn't entertaining at all.

Alright, Ronaldo. You can keep the ten dollars. We're good.
